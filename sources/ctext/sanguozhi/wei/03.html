明皇帝諱叡，字元仲，文帝太子也。生而太祖愛之，常令在左右。
Emperor Mingdi, whose given name was Rui, courtesy name Yuanzhong, was the crown prince of Emperor Wendi. He was loved by Taizu from birth and was often kept in his presence.

明帝紀:
魏書曰：帝生數歲而有岐嶷之姿，武皇帝異之，曰：「我基於爾三世矣。」每朝宴會同，與侍中近臣並列帷幄。好學多識，特留意於法理。年十五，封武德侯，黃初二年為齊公，三年為平原王。以其母誅，故未建為嗣。魏略曰：文帝以郭后無子，詔使子養帝。帝以母不以道終，意甚不平。後不獲已，乃敬事郭后，旦夕因長御問起居，郭后亦自以無子，遂加慈愛。文帝始以帝不恱，有意欲以他姬子京兆王為嗣，故久不拜太子。魏末傳曰：帝常從文帝獵，見子母鹿。文帝射殺鹿母，使帝射鹿子，帝不從，曰：「陛下已殺其母，臣不忍復殺其子。」因涕泣。文帝即放弓箭，以此深奇之，而樹立之意定。七年夏五月，帝病篤，乃立為皇太子。丁巳，即皇帝位，大赦。尊皇太后曰太皇太后，皇后曰皇太后。諸臣封爵各有差。世語曰：帝與朝士素不接，即位之後，羣下想聞風采。居數日，獨見侍中劉曄，語盡日。衆人側聽，曄旣出，問「何如」？曄曰：「秦始皇、漢孝武之儔，才具微不及耳。」癸未，追謚母甄夫人曰文昭皇后。壬辰，立皇弟蕤為陽平王。
The Weishu states: "When the emperor was only several years old, he already displayed extraordinary talent. Emperor Wu was impressed and said, 'My dynasty's foundation lies in you for three generations.'" Whenever there were court banquets or meetings, he would sit alongside the attendants and close ministers within the tented chamber. He was studious and knowledgeable, with a particular interest in legal principles. At the age of fifteen, he was enfeoffed as Hou of Wude. In Huangchu Year 2, he became Gong of Qi; in Huangchu Year 3, he became Wang of Pingyuan. Because his mother was executed, he was not yet established as heir. The Weilue states: "Emperor Wendi, since Consort Guo had no sons, issued an edict ordering her to raise the emperor as her own son." The emperor was greatly distressed because his mother did not die by proper means. Later, with no alternative, he respectfully served Consort Guo. Morning and evening, through the chief eunuch, he inquired about her well-being; Consort Guo also regarded herself as childless and thus showed him kindness and affection. Emperor Wendi initially disapproved of the emperor's lack of joy, and had once considered naming Prince Jingzhao Wang, son of another concubine, as heir; therefore, he delayed for a long time in appointing him crown prince. The Weimochuan states: "The emperor often accompanied Emperor Wendi on hunting expeditions, and once saw a mother deer and her fawn." Emperor Wendi shot and killed the mother deer, then ordered the emperor to shoot the fawn. The emperor refused, saying, "Your Majesty has already killed its mother; I cannot bear to kill its child as well." He wept with emotion. Emperor Wendi immediately put down his bow and arrow, and was deeply impressed by this incident; thus, the decision to establish him as heir was made. In the fifth month of summer in Year 7, when Emperor Wendi's illness worsened, he was appointed crown prince. On the Dingsi day, he ascended the imperial throne and issued a general pardon. He honored the empress dowager as Taihou, and the current queen as Empress Dowager. The various ministers were enfeoffed with marquisates of different ranks. The Shiyu states: "The emperor had never previously interacted closely with the officials at court, and after ascending the throne, those below all wished to hear of his character and demeanor." After several days, he met alone with Attendant-in-Ordinary Liu Ye, speaking throughout the day. The crowd listened from the side; after Liu Ye left, they asked, "How was it?" Liu Ye said: "He is a peer of Qin Shi Huang and Emperor Wu of Han, only slightly less in talent." On the Kuiwei day, he posthumously bestowed the title Wenzhao Empress on his mother, Zhen Fu Ren. On the Rencheng day, he enfeoffed his imperial younger brother Rui as Wang of Yangping.

Emperor Wendi began to take Lady Zhen as a concubine, so Emperor Mingdi should have been born in the tenth year (of Jian'an). Calculating up to this first month, he would have only been thirty-four years old. At that time the calendar was changed; thus, the twelfth month of the previous year was designated as the first month of this new year. It could be 勉强 called the thirty-fifth year, but not the thirty-sixth. On the Kouchou day (March 20, 239 AD), he was buried in Gaoping Ling.

明帝紀:
魏書曰：帝容止可觀，望之儼然。自在東宮，不交朝臣，不問政事，唯潛思書籍而已。即位之後，褒禮大臣，料簡功能，真偽不得相貿，務絕浮華譖毀之端，行師動衆，論決大事，謀臣將相咸服帝之大略。性特彊識，雖左右小臣官簿性行，名跡所履，及其父兄子弟，一經耳目，終不遺忘。含垢藏疾，容受直言，聽受吏民士庶上書，一月之中至數十百封，雖文辭鄙陋，猶覽省究竟，意無猒倦。孫盛曰：聞之長老，魏明帝天姿秀出，立髮垂地，口吃少言，而沉毅好斷。初，諸公受遺輔導，帝皆以方任處之，政自己出。而優禮大臣，開容善直，雖犯顏極諫，無所摧戮，其君人之量如此之偉也。然不思建德垂風，不固維城之基，至使大權偏據，社稷無衞，悲夫！
The Wei Shu states: The emperor had an impressive appearance and dignified bearing; when viewed from afar, he seemed imposing. While in the eastern palace as crown prince, he did not associate with court officials or inquire about government affairs; instead, he devoted himself solely to studying books. After ascending the throne, he honored and treated his senior ministers with propriety, carefully selecting those of ability and merit. Truth and falsehood could not be interchanged; he was determined to eliminate the beginning of superficiality and malicious slander. When mobilizing troops or deciding on major matters, all the strategists, generals, and high officials were convinced by the emperor's grand strategy. By nature, he had an especially strong memory; even the names, official ranks, character, and past records of minor attendants at his side, as well as their fathers, brothers, and younger relatives—once they were mentioned or seen—he would never forget them. He bore grudges and concealed faults, tolerating blunt advice. He listened to memorials from officials, commoners, scholars, and the general populace; in a single month, as many as dozens or even hundreds of letters were submitted. Even if their wording was crude and unrefined, he still carefully read through them all, showing no sign of weariness or aversion. Sun Sheng said: I heard from the elders that Emperor Mingdi of Wei was naturally gifted and outstanding. His hair, when let down, reached to the ground; he had a speech impediment and spoke little, yet he was resolute and decisive in his actions. Initially, when the various ministers were entrusted with advisory roles by imperial decree, the emperor assigned them official positions according to their abilities, but all governance decisions came from himself. Yet he treated his senior ministers with courtesy and openness, welcoming those who spoke the truth. Even if they dared to remonstrate bluntly or offer extreme advice, no one was punished or executed; such was the magnanimity of this ruler. Yet he did not think about cultivating virtue and setting a good example for future generations, nor did he consolidate the foundation of his state. This led to the concentration of great power in one hand, leaving the nation defenseless—how tragic!

明帝紀:
評曰：明帝沉毅斷識，任心而行，蓋有君人之至槩焉。于時百姓彫弊，四海分崩，不先聿脩顯祖，闡拓洪基，而遽追秦皇、漢武，宮館是營，格之遠猷，其殆疾乎！
Commentary: Emperor Mingdi was resolute and discerning, acting according to his own judgment. He truly embodied the highest qualities of a ruler. At that time, the people were exhausted and impoverished, and the realm was in disarray. Instead of first restoring the achievements of his illustrious ancestors and expanding a great foundation, he hastily emulated Qin Shi Huang and Emperor Wu of Han by constructing palaces and pavilions. Judged by long-term strategy, this was surely a grave mistake!
