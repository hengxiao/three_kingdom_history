齊王紀:
魏氏春秋曰：或云任城王楷子。青龍三年，立為齊王。景初三年正月丁亥朔，帝甚病，乃立為皇太子。是日，即皇帝位，大赦。尊皇后曰皇太后。大將軍曹爽、太尉司馬宣王輔政。詔曰：「朕以眇身，繼承鴻業，煢煢在疚，靡所控告。大將軍、太尉奉受末命，夾輔朕躬，司徒、司空、冢宰、元輔總率百僚，以寧社稷，其與羣卿大夫勉勗乃心，稱朕意焉。諸所興作宮室之役，皆以遺詔罷之。官奴婢六十已上，免為良人。」二月，西域重譯獻火浣布，詔大將軍、太尉臨試以示百寮。異物志曰：斯調國有火州，在南海中。其上有野火，春夏自生，秋冬自死。有木生於其中而不消也，枝皮更活，秋冬火死則皆枯瘁。其俗常冬采其皮以為布，色小青黑；若塵垢洿之，便投火中，則更鮮明也。傅子曰：漢桓帝時，大將軍梁兾以火浣布為單衣，常大會賔客，兾陽爭酒，失杯而汙之，偽怒，解衣曰：「燒之。」布得火，煒燁赫然，如燒凡布，垢盡火滅，粲然潔白，若用灰水焉。搜神記曰：崑崙之墟有炎火之山，山上有鳥獸草木，皆生於炎火之中，故有火浣布，非此山草木之皮枲，則其鳥獸之毛也。漢世西域舊獻此布，中間乆絕；至魏初，時人疑其無有。文帝以為火性酷烈，無含生之氣，著之典論，明其不然之事，絕智者之聽。及明帝立，詔三公曰：「先帝昔著典論，不朽之格言，其刊石于廟門之外及太學，與石經並，以永示來世。」至是西域使至而獻火浣布焉，於是刊滅此論，而天下笑之。臣松之昔從征西至洛陽，歷觀舊物，見典論石在太學者尚存，而廟門外無之，問諸長老，云晉初受禪，即用魏廟，移此石於太學，非兩處立也。竊謂此言為不然。又東方朔神異經曰：南荒之外有火山，長三十里，廣五十里，其中皆生不燼之木，晝夜火燒，得暴風不猛，猛雨不滅。火中有鼠，重百斤，毛長二尺餘，細如絲，可以作布。常居火中，色洞赤，時時出外而色白，以水逐而沃之即死，續其毛，織以為布。
Wei Shi Chunqiu said: Some say he was the son of Rencheng Wang Kai. In Qinglong third year, he was established as Qi Wang. On the Dinghai day of the first month, Jinchu third year (February 10, 239 AD), the emperor was very ill and thus appointed him as Crown Prince. On that day, he ascended the imperial throne and issued a general pardon. The empress was honored as Empress Dowager. Grand General Cao Shuang and Grand Commandant Sima Xuanwang assisted in governance. The edict stated: "I, with my humble person, have succeeded to the great legacy. I am alone and burdened with sorrow, having no one to whom I can turn for guidance." "Grand General and Grand Commandant have received my final instructions, assisting me in governance. The Minister of Works, the Minister of Agriculture, the Chief Minister, and the senior ministers shall lead all officials to ensure stability for the state. Wildfires occur there naturally during spring and summer, and die out on their own in autumn and winter. There are trees growing within the fire that do not burn away; their branches and bark remain alive, but they wither when the fires die out in autumn and winter. The local people usually collect its bark in winter to make cloth, which is slightly bluish-black in color; if it becomes dirty or stained, they throw it into the fire, and it then becomes fresh and bright again. Fu Zi said: During the reign of Emperor Huan, Grand General Liang Ji wore a fire-washed cloth single garment. At a grand banquet for guests and visitors, Liang pretended to compete for wine, dropped his cup, and stained it. He feigned anger, took off the robe, and said, "Burn it." When the cloth was exposed to fire, it glowed brilliantly like ordinary fabric being burned. The dirt vanished as the flames died out, leaving it bright and white, just as if it had been cleaned with ashes and water. The Sou Shen Ji stated: "In the ruins of Kunlun, there is a Mountain of Blazing Fire. On this mountain are birds, beasts, grasses, and trees that all grow within the fire. Hence, there is fire-washed cloth; it is not made from the bark or fibers of the plants on this mountain, but rather from the feathers and fur of its birds and animals." In the Han dynasty, emissaries from the Western Regions used to present this cloth, but it had long ceased to be offered; by the beginning of Wei, people at that time doubted whether such a fabric still existed. Emperor Wen believed fire to be fierce and devoid of life, recorded in the Dian Lun, clarifying that such things were not true, thus dismissing them from the ears of the wise. When Emperor Ming ascended the throne, he issued an edict to the Three Dukes: "The late emperor once composed the Dian Lun, a timeless maxim. It should be carved onto stone tablets outside the temple gate and at Taixue, alongside the Stone Classics, to forever demonstrate it to future generations." At this time, emissaries from the Western Regions arrived and presented fire-washed cloth. Thus, this theory was erased, and people across the land ridiculed it. Min Songzhi once followed the western expedition to Luoyang and viewed many historical relics. He saw that stone tablets of Dian Lun at Taixue still remained, but none were found outside the temple gate. When he asked elders about this, they said that in the early years of the Jin dynasty after the transfer of power from Wei, the Jin court used the Wei temple as its own and moved these stone tablets to Taixue; thus, there was no duplication at two locations. I secretly believe this statement is not correct. Also, the Shen Yi Jing by Dongfang 朔 stated: "Beyond the southern wilderness lies a Fire Mountain, thirty li long and fifty li wide. All within it are trees that do not burn, with fire burning day and night; even fierce winds cannot intensify it, and heavy rain cannot extinguish it." Within the fire live rats weighing over one hundred jin, with fur more than two chi long and as fine as silk, which can be made into cloth. They usually live in the fire, appearing bright red; they occasionally emerge outside and appear white. If chased and doused with water, they die. Their fur is then collected and woven into cloth.

坐側。芝出報景王，景王甚歡。又遣使者授齊王印綬，當出就西宮。帝受命，遂載王車，與太后別，垂涕，始從太極殿南出，羣臣送者數十人，太尉司馬孚悲不自勝，餘多流涕。王出後，景王又使使者請璽綬。太后曰：「彭城王，我之季叔也，今來立，我當何之！且明皇帝當絕嗣乎？吾以為高貴鄉公者，文皇帝之長孫，明皇帝之弟子，於禮，小宗有後大宗之義，其詳議之。」景王乃更召群臣，以皇太后令示之，乃定迎高貴鄉公。是時太常已發二日，待璽綬於溫。事定，又請璽綬。太后令曰：「我見高貴鄉公，小時識之，明日我自欲以璽綬手授之也。」
On the Gengxu day (April 2, 254 AD), Zhongshu Ling Li Feng conspired with Zhang Ji, the father of the Empress and Guanglu Dafu, to depose high-ranking officials and appoint Xiahou Xuan, the Taichang, as Da Jiangjun. When the plot was discovered, all those involved were executed. On the Gengxu day following (April 3, 254 AD) a general amnesty was declared. In March, Empress Zhang was deposed. In the fourth month of summer, Empress Wang was installed, and a general amnesty was proclaimed. In the fifth month, the emperor bestowed titles upon the Empress's father: Fengche Duyi Wang Kui was enfeoffed as Guangming Xiang Hou and appointed Guanglu Dafu with a special rank of Tejin. His wife Tian Shi was honored as Xuanyang Xiang Jun. In the ninth month of autumn, Da Jiangjun Sima Jingwang planned to depose the emperor and informed the Empress Dowager. Both Shiyu and Wei Shi Chunqiu record: In this autumn, Jiang Wei raided Longyou. At that time, Andong Jiangjun Sima Wenwang was stationed in Xuchang. He was summoned back to attack Wei and arrived at the capital. The emperor observed his troops passing by from Pingluo Guan. Zhong Lingjun Xu Yun and some minor court officials plotted to take advantage of Sima Wenwang's farewell ceremony, kill him, and command his troops to force the Da Jiangjun out. The imperial decree had already been written beforehand. When Sima Wenwang entered, the emperor was just eating chestnuts. The court entertainers Yun Wu and others sang: "Green-headed chicken, green-headed chicken." A green-headed chicken is actually a duck. The emperor was frightened and dared not act. Sima Wenwang led his troops into the city, and Sima Jingwang took advantage of this to plot the emperor's deposition. Min Songzhi notes: According to the Xiahou Xuan Zhuan and Wei Lue, Xu Yun was involved in this year's spring plot with Li Feng. After Li Feng was executed, Xu Yun was immediately transferred to serve as Zhenbei Jiangjun. Before he could depart, however, he was arrested for misappropriating official property and handed over to Tingwei. He was exiled to Lelang but was intercepted and killed en route. At that time in autumn, Xu Yun could not have been the Lingjun and thus plotted this course of action. On the Jia Xu day (October 10, 254 AD), the Empress Dowager issued an edict: "The emperor Fang has reached adulthood but does not personally attend to state affairs. He indulges in the favor of concubines and is immersed in feminine vices, daily entertaining musicians and jesters while allowing their vulgar antics. He has invited family members of the Six Palaces to stay in his private chambers, thereby destroying the order of human relationships and corrupting the moral distinctions between men and women; His filial piety diminishes daily, while arrogance grows increasingly severe. He is no longer fit to inherit the Mandate of Heaven or serve as ruler in ancestral temples. Therefore, let Gao Rou, concurrently serving as Taivai, present the decree and announce it with the title "Yiyuan DAWU" at the ancestral temples. Send Emperor Fang back to Qi as a vassal ruler so he may step aside from the imperial throne." The Wei Shu records: On this day, Sima Jingwang carried out the Empress Dowager's order and summoned the gongqing and senior ministers of the central court for a conference. The ministers were visibly shocked. Sima Jingwang wept and said, "The Empress Dowager has given such an order; what will you all do for the royal house!" They all replied, "In the past, Yi Yin exiled Taijia to pacify the Shang dynasty and Huo Guang deposed Changyi Wang to stabilize the Han. To use authority decisively for the sake of the state and save the people is a practice followed by two dynasties in ancient times. Now it falls upon you, Your Excellence. Today's matter depends entirely on your command." Sima Jingwang said, "Since all of you place such great expectations upon me as a teacher and leader, where could I possibly hide from this responsibility?" The achievements of the three ancestors' virtue shine across the six directions. The emperor has ascended the throne and inherited a great legacy. Now that he is of age, he does not personally attend to affairs of state but indulges in concubines' favor and sinks into feminine vices. He neglects lectures on learning and disgraces Confucian scholars. Every day, he invites minor entertainers such as Guo Huai and Yuan Xin to perform at the front of Jianshi Furong Dian, where they appear nude and play games, allowing them to consort with palace ladies like Baolin Nüshang in disorderly ways. The emperor personally leads the inner court women to watch these performances. Moreover, at Guangwang Guan, he had Huai and Xin perform the "Liaodong Y 妖妇" (a lewd performance) beneath the pavilion. Their indecent behavior was so excessive that passersby covered their eyes in disgust, while the emperor watched from above, laughing and feasting on such vulgarities. In the secluded courtyards of Lingyun Tai, he set up curtains and invited nine female relatives. The emperor watched from Xuanqu Guan as he ordered Huai and Xin to enter the curtained area with them for drinking sessions. Huai, Xin, and others circulated among the women pouring wine until they were all drunk, engaging in indecent and disrespectful behavior with no distinction. He also had Baolin Li Hua, Liu Xun, and others play with Huai and Xin. Qingshang Ling Linghu Jing rebuked Hua and Xun, saying: "These women are the emperor's attendants; they hold official positions. How can you behave in such a manner?" Hua and Xun repeatedly slandered Linghu Jing. The emperor often enjoyed shooting people with a slingshot, which greatly angered Linghu Jing; he shot at Jing without avoiding his head or eyes. Jing said to the emperor: "The late emperor strictly guarded the court's discipline, but now Your Majesty daily indulges in unrestrained play with empresses and consorts, even watching entertainers perform indecently while appearing nude. This must not be allowed to reach the Empress Dowager's ears. Jing does not fear death; he speaks only for Your Majesty's sake." The emperor said, "Am I the Son of Heaven and yet cannot act freely?" "What business is it of the Empress Dowager!" He then ordered men to burn red-hot iron and use it to scorch Jing, causing his entire body to blister. After Empress Zhen passed away, the emperor wished to appoint Consort Wang as empress. The Empress Dowager, however, wanted to choose someone from outside the palace. The emperor angrily told Jing and others: "In previous generations of Wei, when empresses were chosen, they were always selected according to my wishes. If the Empress Dowager insists on going against my will, does she know I am about to go?" Later, he indeed treated Empress Zhang with coldness and neglect. When the Empress Dowager mourned for Heyang Jun, the emperor remained in the back garden daily, enjoying music and performances as usual, rarely visiting her to express filial concern. Qingshang Cheng Pang Xi advised the emperor: "The Empress Dowager is extremely filial. Now she suffers great sorrow and refuses to take even water or food. Your Majesty should visit her frequently to comfort her; you cannot merely remain here indulging in music." The emperor said, "I will do as I please. Who can stop me?" When the Empress Dowager returned to Beigong, she ordered the execution of Consort Zhang and Yu Wan. The emperor was furious and told Jing and others: "The Empress Dowager arbitrarily killed those I loved; there is no longer any mother-son affection." He visited their former quarters many times to weep in grief, secretly ordering the Burial Office to provide a lavish coffin and burial, ensuring that the Empress Dowager would not learn of it. Whenever he saw female relatives with beautiful appearances, he sometimes detained them and handed them over to Qingshang. The emperor played in the bamboo groves of the back garden or walked hand-in-hand with his attendants. Pang Xi reported: "It is inappropriate for attendants to walk hand in hand with the Son of Heaven." The emperor became angry and once again shot at Pang Xi with his slingshot. He spent each day wandering in the back garden, and whenever official documents were brought in from outside, he never reviewed them. When his attendants said "Take it out," he did not even bother to look at them. The Empress Dowager ordered the emperor to regularly attend lectures in Shiqian Dian, but he refused and instead sent others. The emperor simply left without attending. When the Empress Dowager came to inquire about him, he always had Huangmen officials falsely reply that "He is here." Jing and Xi, fearing for their lives, no longer dared to stop him but instead joined in flattery. The emperor indulged freely in corruption and lust, destroying the order of human relations and corrupting moral distinctions between men and women. His filial piety deteriorated further while his wickedness grew increasingly severe. We, your ministers, fear the collapse of the empire and the endangerment of the state; even if we were to die, it would not be enough to atone for our failure. Now that the emperor is unfit to inherit Heaven's mandate, we ministers request, following the precedent of Han dynasty official Huo Guang, to seize his imperial seal and sash. The emperor originally ascended the throne as the Prince of Qi; he should return to his fief in Qi. Let Sikou Minister Rou hold the imperial scepter and, together with relevant officials, offer a grand sacrifice to the ancestral temples using the "tai lao" ritual. We respectfully submit this report at the risk of our lives." The memorial was approved. On that day, the emperor moved to a different palace and was twenty-three years old. Envoys bearing imperial scepters escorted him, while preparations were made for the Prince of Qi's palace at Chongmen in Hedong. The arrangements followed the etiquette and standards appropriate to a vassal state. The Wei Lue records: When Sima Jingwang was about to depose the emperor, he sent Guo Zhi into the palace to inform the Empress Dowager; at that time, the Empress Dowager and the emperor were seated facing each other. Zhi told the emperor: "The Da Jiangjun wants to depose Your Majesty and install Prince Peicheng Ju as emperor." Upon hearing this, the emperor immediately rose and left. The Empress Dowager was displeased. Zhi said, "The Empress Dowager had a son but failed to educate him. Now the Da Jiangjun's decision is final; he has already stationed troops outside in preparation for any emergency. You should simply comply with his orders and have nothing more to say!" The Empress Dowager said, "I wish to see the Da Jiangjun in person and speak to him." Zhi replied, "How could that be allowed?" "You should only act quickly to seize the imperial seal and sash." The Empress Dowager yielded in spirit, so she sent a nearby attendant to retrieve the imperial seal and place it beside her. Zhi exited and reported this to Sima Jingwang, who was very pleased. They also sent envoys to bestow the seals and sash of the Prince of Qi upon him, ordering that he leave for the western palace. The emperor accepted the order and boarded his princely carriage, bidding farewell to the Empress Dowager with tears. He departed from the southern gate of Taiji Dian; dozens of ministers accompanied him on this journey. Taivai Sima Fu was overcome by grief, while many others wept as well. After the prince departed, Sima Jingwang sent another envoy to request the imperial seal and sash once more. The Empress Dowager replied, "Prince Peicheng is my younger uncle. If he were to be installed as emperor now, where would I go?" "And must the descendants of Emperor Ming truly die out?" "I believe that Gao Gui Xianggong is the eldest grandson of Emperor Wen and a nephew of Emperor Ming. According to ritual, there is the principle that a junior branch may succeed a senior one. You should deliberate on this matter carefully." Sima Jingwang then summoned the ministers again, showing them the Empress Dowager's decree, and thus decided to proceed with welcoming Gao Gui Xianggong. At that time, the Taichang had already departed two days earlier, waiting for the imperial seal and sash at Wen. Once the matter was settled, they requested the imperial seal and sash again. The Empress Dowager issued a decree: "I have seen Gao Gui Xianggong since he was young and recognize him. Tomorrow, I myself wish to hand the imperial seal and sash directly into his hands."

There is no need for them to return, in order to establish a broad and trustworthy reputation."

Commentary: In ancient times, the empire was regarded as public property and given only to those who were virtuous. Later generations passed on their positions through hereditary succession, establishing the legitimate son as heir; If there was no legitimate heir to succeed, then one should choose a close relative of virtuous character, as in the case of Emperor Wen and Emperor Xuan of Han—this has always been an unchangeable standard. Emperor Ming was unable to follow this principle, and instead allowed his emotions to be bound by personal affection. He raised an infant and passed on the great throne to him, but did not entrust it exclusively; he necessarily involved collateral relatives. In the end, Cao Shuang was executed, and Emperor Qi lost his position. Gao Gui Gong possessed early-developed talent and wisdom, loved to ask questions and valued eloquence—truly embodying the refined style of Emperor Wen. Yet he was light-minded, impetuous, and prone to anger; as a result, he brought upon himself great disaster. Chenliu Wang, seated in the southern court with reverence and self-restraint, entrusted governance to his prime ministers. He followed precedent by yielding power through courtesy and mutual respect, thus receiving a grand fiefdom as guest of Jin. Compared to Shanyang, he was even more honored and favored.
