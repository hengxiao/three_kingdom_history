荀彧傳:
荀彧字文若，潁川潁陰人也。祖父淑，字季和，朗陵令。當漢順、桓之間，知名當世。有子八人，號曰八龍。彧父緄，濟南相。叔父爽，司空。
Xun Yu, courtesy name Wenruo, was a native of Yingyin, Yingtong Commandery. His grandfather Xun Shu, courtesy name Jihe, served as the magistrat of Langling. During the reigns of Emperor Shun and Emperor Huan of the Han dynasty, he was well known in his time. He had eight sons, who were called the "Eight Dragons." Xun Yu's father was Xun Jian, who served as xiang of Jining. His uncle Xun Shuang held the post of Sikong (Minister of Works).

荀彧傳:
續漢書曰：淑有高才，王暢、李膺皆以為師，為朗陵侯相，號稱神君。張璠漢紀曰：淑博學有高行，與李固、李膺同志友善，拔李昭於小吏，友黃叔度於幼童，以賢良方正徵，對策譏切梁氏，出補朗陵侯相，卒官。八子：儉、緄、靖、燾、詵、爽、肅、旉。音敷。爽字慈明，幼好學，年十二，通春秋、論語，耽思經典，不應徵命，積十數年。董卓秉政，復徵爽，爽欲遁去，吏持之急。詔下郡，即拜平原相。行至苑陵，又追拜光祿勳。視事三日，策拜司空。爽起自布衣，九十五日而至三公。淑舊居西豪里，縣令苑康曰昔高陽氏有才子八人，署其里為高陽里。靖字叔慈，亦有至德，名幾亞爽，隱居終身。皇甫謐逸士傳：或問許子將，靖與爽孰賢？子將曰：「二人皆玉也，慈明外朗，叔慈內潤。」
The Xuhanshu states: "Shu had lofty talent. Wang Chang and Li Ying both regarded him as their teacher. He served as xiang of Marquis Langling, and was known as the Divine Lord." Zhang Fan's Han Ji states: "Shu was broadly learned and of high moral character. He shared the same ideals as Li Gu and Li Ying, and maintained friendly relations with them. He promoted Li Zhao from a minor official position and befriended Huang Shudu when he was still a child. Summoned for an imperial examination on the basis of his virtue and integrity, he submitted answers that sharply criticized the Liang clan. Later, he left to serve as xiang of Marquis Langiling, where he died in office." The eight sons were: Jian, Jian, Jing, Dao, Shen, Shuang, Su, and Fu. Pinyin: Fu. Shuang, courtesy name Ciming, was fond of learning from an early age. At the age of twelve, he had mastered Chunqiu and Lunyu. He devoted himself to studying classical texts and refused official appointments for more than a decade. When Dong Zhuo assumed power, he again summoned Shuang. Shuang wanted to escape, but the officials held him tightly. An imperial edict was issued to his native commandery, and he was immediately appointed xiang of Pingyuan. While en route to Yuanling, he received another imperial appointment as Guanglu Xun. Three days after assuming office, he was conferred the title of Sikong (Minister of Public Works) by imperial decree. Shuang rose from commoner to a Three-People official in just ninety-five days. Shu's former residence was located in Xihao Li. The county magistrate Yuan Kang said, "In the past, Gao Yang Shi had eight talented sons," and renamed his neighborhood as Gaoyang Li. Jing, courtesy name Shuci, also possessed great virtue; he was nearly as famous as Shuang and lived in seclusion for his entire life. Huangfu Mi's Yishi Zhuan: "Someone asked Xu Zijiang, which of Jing or Shuang was the more virtuous?" Zijiang said, "Both men are like jade. Ciming shines outwardly, while Shuci is inwardly refined."

Commentary: Xun Yu was refined, elegant, and erudite, possessing the style of a statesman who could assist a king. However, though he had foresight and insight into opportunities, his abilities were not fully realized in fulfilling his ambitions. Many people of the time criticized Xun Yu for cooperating with the Wei clan and thereby undermining the Han dynasty's mandate; the change in ruler-subject relations was indeed due to Xun Yu. Although he later distinguished himself, it could not prevent the shifting of fate; his achievements were at odds with righteousness, and his insight was also a source of regret. Chen's evaluation is essentially the same as that of the general consensus. I, Songzhi, believe these remarks fail to grasp the broader and more profound aspects of the matter. Could Xun Yu not have known that Cao Cao's ambitions were not those of a loyal minister to the declining Han dynasty? This was because at that time, the royal way had already weakened, chaos spread widely, and ambitious men eyed power like tigers; everyone harbored different intentions. Without a capable leader to quell disorder and a strategy based on righteousness, the Han dynasty would have suddenly collapsed, and the common people would have been annihilated. If one wishes to assist a contemporary hero in restoring order during times of turmoil, who else could be more suitable than such a person? Therefore, in addressing urgent crises as one would save their own life, he was able to act decisively amidst peril and achieve great success. The common people were thus rescued like passengers on a boat, and the Liu clan's rule extended for another two generations. Was this not Xun Yu's original aspiration and the far-reaching result of his benevolence and mercy? It was only when the hegemonic cause had already flourished and the Han dynasty's traces were clearly being erased that he chose to sacrifice his life for his principles, expressing his lifelong sincerity. By upholding righteousness in his time and spreading sincerity through generations, he can truly be said to have shouldered a heavy responsibility and pursued a distant path, with steadfast aspirations and established virtue. To say that his ambitions were unfulfilled is surely a false accusation! Xun You and Jia Xu, their strategies were almost flawless in calculation, demonstrating profound understanding and adaptability. Were they not the equals of Zhang Liang and Chen Ping? I, Songzhi, consider that the structure of biographical records should group individuals according to their similar deeds. Zhang Zifang was a man of lofty virtue, truly not comparable to Chen Ping. Yet among the Han dynasty's strategists, Zhang Liang and Chen Ping were the only ones. If they are not grouped together, then others have no one to associate with; therefore, previous historians combined them, which was indeed appropriate. Among Wei's contemporaries comparable to Xu, there were many more. Yet Xu was not grouped with Cheng and Guo, but instead listed alongside the two Xuns; this is a misclassification. Moreover, as for the characters of You and Xu, are they not like a night pearl compared to an ordinary candle! Although both provide illumination, their qualities differ greatly. Nowadays, the evaluation of Xun and Jia is given a single title; this especially fails to distinguish between them appropriately.
