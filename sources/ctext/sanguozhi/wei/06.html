董卓傳:
英雄記曰：卓父君雅，由微官為潁川綸氏尉。有三子：長子擢，字孟高，早卒；次即卓；卓弟旻字叔穎。少好俠，甞游羌中，盡與諸豪帥相結。後歸耕於野，而豪帥有來從之者，卓與俱還，殺耕牛與相宴樂。諸豪帥感其意，歸相斂，得雜畜千餘頭以贈卓。吳書曰：郡召卓為吏，使監領盜賊。胡甞出鈔，多虜民人，涼州刺史成就辟卓為從事，使領兵騎討捕，大破之，斬獲千計。并州刺史段熲薦卓公府，司徒袁隗辟為掾。漢桓帝末，以六郡良家子為羽林郎。卓有才武，旅力少比，雙帶兩鞬，左右馳射。為軍司馬，從中郎將張奐征并州有功，拜郎中，賜縑九千匹，卓悉以分與吏士。遷廣武令，蜀郡北部都尉，西域戊己校尉，免。徵拜并州刺史、河東太守，英雄記曰：卓數討羌、胡，前後百餘戰。遷中郎將，討黃巾，軍敗抵罪。韓遂等起涼州，復為中郎將，西拒遂。於望垣硤北為羌、胡數萬人所圍，糧食乏絕。卓偽欲捕魚，堰其還道當所渡水為池，使水渟滿數十里，默從堰下過其軍而決堰。比羌、胡聞知追逐，水已深，不得渡。時六軍上隴西，五軍敗績，卓獨全衆而還，屯住扶風。拜前將軍，封斄鄉侯，徵為并州牧。靈帝紀曰：中平五年，徵卓為少府，勑以營吏士屬左將軍皇甫嵩，詣行在所。卓上言：「涼州擾亂，鯨鯢未滅，此臣奮發效命之秋。吏士踊躍，戀恩念報，各遮臣車，辭聲懇惻，未得即路也。輙且行前將軍事，盡心慰卹，効力行陣。」六年，以卓為并州牧，又勑以吏兵屬皇甫嵩。卓復上言：「臣掌戎十年，士卒大小相狎彌乆，戀臣畜養之恩，樂為國家奮一旦之命，乞將之州，效力邊陲。」卓再違詔勑，會為何進所召。
The Records of Heroes states: Dong Zhuo's father, Junya, rose from a minor official to become the magistracy officer (wei) of Lunshi in Yingchuan. He had three sons: his eldest son, Zhuo, courtesy name Menggao, died at an early age; the second was Zhuo himself; Dong Zhuo's younger brother was Min, courtesy name Shuying. He was young and fond of chivalry; he once traveled in the Qiang region and formed connections with all the local chieftains. Later, he returned to farming in the countryside, and when some of the chieftains came to follow him, Dong Zhuo brought them back together, slaughtered oxen for plowing, and entertained them with feasts and pleasures. The chieftains were moved by his gesture and returned to collect contributions, presenting Dong Zhuo with over a thousand miscellaneous livestock as a gift. The Book of Wu states: The commandery summoned Dong Zhuo to serve as an official and assigned him to supervise the suppression of bandits. The Hu people once launched a raid, capturing many civilians. The governor of Liangzhou, Chengjiu, appointed Dong Zhuo as an assistant official and ordered him to lead cavalry troops in pursuit and suppression; he defeated them greatly, killing or capturing thousands. The governor of Bingzhou, Duan Jing, recommended Dong Zhuo to the imperial court; the Grand Tutor Yuan Wei appointed him as a clerk. In the later years of Emperor Huan of Han, he was appointed as a Youlin Lang because he was the son of an upstanding family from one of the six commanderies. Dong Zhuo possessed talent and martial prowess, with physical strength rarely matched; he wore two quivers across his waist and could ride and shoot skillfully on both sides. Serving as a military Sima, he followed the Zhonglangjiang Zhang Huan on an expedition to Bingzhou and achieved notable success. He dismounted and removed his saddle, seeing that Gongsun Zan had already been defeated, so he did not make any preparations for defense. Only a few dozen strong crossbowmen and more than one hundred halberdiers accompanied him personally. Suddenly, over two thousand of Gongsun Zan's scattered cavalry arrived and immediately surrounded Yuan Shao in several layers, arrows raining down like rain. Yuan Shao's assistant officer Tian Feng supported Yuan Shao and tried to lead him into an empty walled area for shelter, but Yuan Shao struck the ground with his helmet and said: "A true man should fight and die at the front; how can one survive by hiding behind a wall?" The strong crossbows then fired in disorder, killing and wounding many. Gongsun Zan's cavalry did not realize it was Yuan Shao they were attacking, so they gradually withdrew; Just then Qu Yi arrived to rescue him, and the enemy forces dispersed. Whenever Gongsun Zan fought against the northern tribes, he often rode a white horse. His arrows never missed their mark, and he frequently achieved victories over the enemy. The nomads would warn each other: "We must avoid the White Horse." Because of this fear among the northern tribes, Gongsun Zan selected several thousand white horses and chose skilled mounted archers to form a unit known as the White Horse Volunteers; Another account says that among the Hu and Yi tribes, strong warriors often rode white horses. Gongsun Zan also had several thousand strong cavalrymen who mostly rode white horses; thus, he adopted this title for them. After defeating Gongsun Zan, Yuan Shao led his army south to Boluojin. While he was holding a meeting with his guests and generals, he received news that the Hui Jun forces had rebelled and joined forces with the Black Mountain bandit Yu Du to attack Ye City, killing its governor Li Cheng in the process. The rebels were divided into more than ten groups, with a total of tens of thousands of people gathering around Ye City. Among the guests present, some had families in Ye and were filled with anxiety and fear, their faces pale. Some even stood up weeping; yet Yuan Shao remained calm, his expression unchanged, composed as ever. Among the rebels was Tao Sheng, a former minor official of Neihuang who had a kind heart. Alone among them, he led his followers over the western city wall and entered, closing and guarding the state gate to keep out other bandits. He loaded Yuan Shao's family members and all the gentry within the state onto carts, personally protecting them as they were sent to Chiqiu before returning. When Yuan Shao arrived, he stationed his forces at Chiqiu and appointed Tao Sheng as Jianyi Zhonglangjiang (General of the Established Righteousness). Yuan Shao then led his army into the Cangyan Gorge of Luchang Mountain in Chaoge to attack Yu Du. After a five-day siege, he defeated them, killing Yu Du and Hu Shou, who had been appointed by Chang'an as the Inspector of Ji Province. He then continued northward along the mountain route and launched a sudden attack on various rebel leaders, including Zuozi Zhangba, killing them all. He also attacked Liu Shi, Qing Niujiao, Huanglong, Zuo Xiao, Guo Daxian, Li Damu, and Yu Digeng. He razed their camps to the ground, driving the survivors into flight with many captured or killed; tens of thousands were beheaded. Yuan Shao returned once more and stationed his army at Ye. In the fourth year of Chuping, the Son of Heaven sent Tai Fu Ma Ridi and Taifu Zhao Qi to mediate in Guandong. Zhao Qi separately went to Hebei, where Yuan Shao met him more than a hundred li away and paid homage, conveying the imperial decree. Zhao Qi stayed in Yuan Shao's camp and sent a letter to Gongsun Zan. Gongsun Zan sent an envoy to write back to Yuan Shao, saying: "Zhao Taifu, with the virtue of Zhou and Zhao, has been entrusted with a mission to come here. He proclaims the imperial grace and shows us harmony; it is as if clouds have parted to reveal the sun—how joyful this is!" In the past, Jia Fu and Kou Xun also quarreled over their soldiers and wanted to harm each other. But they encountered Emperor Guangwu's clemency; he personally summoned them both for an audience at court, allowing them to travel together in the same carriage. At that time, people considered this a great honor. Reflecting on my humble position at the frontier, I am now able to share this blessing with General Shao. This is truly a kindness from you, and a great fortune for Zan." Later, Qu Yi became arrogant and overbearing due to his achievements, so Yuan Shao had him killed.

Initially, the enthronement of the Son of Heaven was not in accordance with Yuan Shao's wishes. When he was stationed at Hedong, Yuan Shao sent Guo Tu of Yingchuan from Yingtian to serve as an envoy there. After returning, Guo Tu advised Yuan Shao to welcome the Son of Heaven and establish his capital at Ye, but Yuan Shao did not agree. The biography of Emperor Xian records: Ju Shou advised Yuan Shao, saying, "General, your family has for generations served as a loyal and righteous support to the throne. Now, the imperial court is displaced and the ancestral temples destroyed; looking at various states and commanderies, they outwardly claim to raise righteous armies but inwardly plot each other's destruction. There are none who remain loyal to the Son of Heaven or care for the people. Moreover, now that the state's cities are roughly pacified, we should welcome the Son of Heaven to establish his court in Ye. By holding the emperor as a figurehead and issuing commands to the feudal lords, while training soldiers and horses to punish those who defy us, who could possibly resist?" Yuan Shao was pleased and intended to follow this advice. Guo Tu and Chunyu Qiong said, "The Han dynasty has been declining for a long time. Now you wish to revive it—would that not be difficult! Moreover, now heroes hold the states and commanderies with forces numbering in the tens of thousands. This is what is meant by 'When Qin lost its deer, whoever captured it first became king.' If we welcome the Son of Heaven into our territory, every move will require a report to him. If we follow his orders, our authority is weakened; if we disobey, we are defying imperial commands. This would not be a wise strategy." Ju Shou replied, "Now is the time to welcome the imperial court—this would be both righteous and in line with the greater strategy. If we do not act quickly, others will surely take this initiative before us. Opportunity must not be missed; success lies in swift action. General, you should consider this carefully!" Yuan Shao did not follow the advice. According to this record, which attributes the strategy to Guo Tu, it contradicts Yuan Shao's official biography. At that time, Cao Cao welcomed the Son of Heaven and established his capital at Xu, securing the lands south of the Yellow River, with all of Guanzhong submitting to him. Yuan Shao regretted his decision and wanted Cao Cao to move the Son of Heaven's capital to Juancheng, so as to be closer himself. However, Cao Cao refused this request. The Son of Heaven appointed Yuan Shao as Taivue, later changing his title to Dajiangjun and bestowing upon him the title of Ye Hou. According to Chunqiu of Emperor Xian: Yuan Shao was ashamed that he ranked below Cao Cao and angrily said; "Cao Cao has deserved death many times, yet I have repeatedly saved him. Now he dares to betray my kindness and hold the Son of Heaven as a pawn to command me!" Cao Cao heard this, and offered the title of Dajiangjun to Yuan Shao. Yuan Shao declined the marquisate but accepted the other titles. After a short while, he defeated Gongsun Zan at Yijing and captured his troops. Dianlue states: From this point on, Yuan Shao's tribute to the imperial court became infrequent and disrespectful. He secretly sent his chief clerk Geng Bao to report privately, saying, "The virtue of the red mandate has waned completely; Yuan is a descendant of yellow, and should follow Heaven's will." Yuan Shao showed Geng Bao's secret report to the military officers and officials in his administration. Those who discussed it all considered Geng Bao a superstitious fraud deserving of execution, so Yuan Shao killed him to absolve himself. Jiuzhou Chunqiu states: Yuan Shao invited and summoned Zheng Xuan of Beihai but did not treat him with proper respect. Zhao Rong, upon hearing this, said, "A virtuous man is the hope of gentlemen. To fail to honor a worthy person is to lose the hopes of gentlemen." A true and capable ruler dares not lose the goodwill of the people; how much less should one do so with regard to gentlemen? To lose the hopes of gentlemen, it is difficult indeed for a ruler to achieve anything meaningful." The Records of Heroes records that Cao Cao composed a song about Dong Zhuo, with these lines: "Virtue and conduct remain unblemished; yet change and misfortune are hard to avoid. Zheng Kangcheng served wine, fell to the ground and died of suffocation; Guo Jingtu's life ended beneath a mulberry tree in his garden." According to such wording, Zheng Xuan passed away suddenly without illness. Other records do not mention this, so I have included it here for reference. Yuan Shao sent his eldest son Yuan Tan to serve as governor of Qingzhou, but Ju Shou advised him, saying: "This will surely be the beginning of disaster." Yuan Shao did not listen and said, "I wish for each of my sons to hold a state." Jiuzhou Chunqiu records Ju Shou's words of advice as follows: "The world says that when a single rabbit runs down an intersection, ten thousand people chase it. But once one person catches it, the greedy all stop—because their shares are now determined. Moreover, when ages and abilities are equal, virtue is used to decide; if virtues are also equal, divination is resorted to—this was the ancient system. I hope you will consider above the lessons of past successes and failures from previous dynasties, and below reflect on the principle that when a rabbit is caught, its share is determined." Yuan Shao said, "I wish for my four sons to each hold one state so I can observe their abilities." Ju Shou stepped out and said, "Is this the beginning of disaster!" Yuan Tan first arrived in Qingzhou as a military commander and had not yet been appointed governor. Later, Cao Cao bestowed the title of governor upon him. The territory under his control westward from the Yellow River probably did not extend beyond Pingyuan alone. He then moved north to expel Tian Kai and attacked Kong Rong in the east, displaying his military might at the edge of the sea. At that time, with no ruler for the people, they joyfully supported him. However, he placed trust in a group of petty men and favored flatterers, indulging his desires in luxury and excess without understanding the hardships of farming. Hua Yan and Kong Shun were both treacherous flatterers, yet Yuan Tan trusted them as his closest confidants; Wang Xiu and others were merely kept in official positions. Nevertheless, he was capable of receiving guests and admired the reputation of virtuous men, showing them respect. He allowed his brother-in-law to command troops within the territory, leading to banditry; outside of markets and streets, fields were plundered. He also sent two generals to recruit soldiers from the counties, exempting those who bribed them while seizing resources from those who did not. Since most people were poor and weak, many fled into hills and fields, where troops hunted for them like birds and beasts in a hunt. In towns with ten thousand households, the registered population rarely exceeded several hundred; when collecting taxes and levies, less than one-third actually entered state coffers. When he summoned virtuous men to office, they refused to come forward; those who did not forcefully abandon their military duties but instead remained at ease among their clans and fellow townspeople could also not be punished. He also appointed his second son Yuan Xi as governor of Youzhou, and his nephew Gao Gan as governor of Bingzhou. With an army of hundreds of thousands, he entrusted Shen Pei and Feng Ji with military command, appointed Tian Feng, Xun Chen, and Xu You as his chief strategists, and named Yan Liang and Wen Chou as generals. He selected ten thousand elite infantry and a thousand cavalry to prepare for an attack on Xu. Shiyu states: Yuan Shao had fifty thousand foot soldiers and eight thousand cavalrymen. Sun Sheng commented: According to Cao Wei Wu's statement to Cui Yan, "Yesterday I examined the household registers of your state and found that thirty thousand troops could be raised." From this we can infer, if only the able-bodied men of Ji Province were sufficient for such numbers, how much more so when combined with You, Bing, and Qing provinces? Yuan Shao's major campaign must have mobilized his entire force; the number of ten thousand is close to accurate. The biography of Emperor Xian states: When Yuan Shao prepared to lead his army south, Ju Shou and Tian Feng advised him, saying, "Our troops have been on campaign for many years; the people are exhausted, granaries are empty, taxes and labor duties remain heavy—this is a deep concern for our state. We should first send envoys to report our victories to the Son of Heaven, focus on agriculture, and allow the people rest; If this is not possible, we should then report that the Cao family has blocked our access to the Son of Heaven. Afterward, we may station troops at Liyang and gradually prepare for operations in the south of the Yellow River. We should build more boats, repair weapons and equipment, send out elite cavalry detachments to raid their borders, making them unable to rest while we take advantage of their exhaustion. In three years' time, the matter can be settled without difficulty." Shen Pei and Guo Tu said, "According to military strategy, ten times the enemy's strength allows for encirclement; five times allows for attack. When forces are equal, one can still fight. Now with your illustrious might and the powerful forces across the Hebei region, we should attack Cao's faction. It is like turning over one's palm; if we do not seize this opportunity now, it will be difficult to act later." Ju Shou said: "To rescue the people from chaos and punish tyranny is called a righteous army; to rely on numbers and strength alone is to become an arrogant, reckless force. An army acting in righteousness will be invincible; a proud one will fall first. Cao Cao has welcomed the Son of Heaven and settled his court at Xudu. Now, if we raise our army to march southward, it would be a violation of righteousness. Moreover, the strategy for victory in battle does not lie solely in strength or weakness. Cao Cao's laws and commands are already well enforced, his soldiers disciplined and trained—they are not like Gongsun Zan who was defeated while sitting in a siege. Now, abandoning the sure path to safety and raising an army without just cause—this secretly fills me with fear for you!" Guo Tu and others said, "King Wu of Zhou attacked King Zhou and it was not called unjust; how much less can an attack on Cao Cao be considered without cause! Moreover, your troops and officers are all exerting their strength with determination; the soldiers and generals are filled with anger and ambition, eager to prove themselves. If we do not seize this moment to establish our great cause in time, it will be a grave mistake. Heaven offers an opportunity but if one does not take it, he will suffer the consequences. This is why Yue became dominant and Wu perished. The strategy of the military supervisor (Ju Shou) lies in caution, not in recognizing the right moment or adapting to changing circumstances." Yuan Shao followed their advice. Guo Tu and others then slandered Ju Shou, saying: "He oversees both internal and external affairs and commands great authority over the three armies. If his power continues to grow, how can we control him? When a minister and ruler differ in purpose, they prosper; when the ruler and minister are aligned, they perish. This is what Huang Shi feared. Moreover, one who commands troops outside should not be involved in internal affairs." Yuan Shao became doubtful. He then divided the command of the military supervisors into three generals, assigning Ju Shou and Guo Tu, as well as Chunyu Qiong, each to lead one army. Thus they united and marched southward.

When one man raised his arm in defiance, an entire province echoed in support. The Dian Lun says: Yuan Tan is the elder son with wisdom, while Yuan Shang is the younger and handsome. Yuan Shao's wife, Lady Liu, favored Yuan Shang and often praised his talents. Yuan Shao also found him striking in appearance and wished to name him as heir; however, before this was made official, Yuan Shao died. Lady Liu was known for her cruel jealousy. After Yuan Shao's death, before his body had been buried, she killed all five of his favored concubines. She believed that the dead could be aware and would meet Yuan Shao again in the afterlife, so she shaved their heads, blackened their faces, and disfigured them. Yuan Shang then had all the families of those who were killed put to death as well. Shen Pei and Feng Ji vied for power with Xin Ping and Guo Tu. Shen Pei and Feng Ji supported Yuan Shang, while Xin Ping and Guo Tu backed Yuan Tan. The majority of the people favored Yuan Tan as the elder son and wished to support him as ruler. Shen Pei et al., fearing that Yuan Tan's ascension would lead to Xin Ping and others becoming a threat, followed Yuan Shao's previous intentions and thus placed Yuan Shang on the throne in succession. When Yuan Tan arrived, he was not allowed to assume power and instead styled himself as General of Chaji (Chariot and Cavalry). From this point on, a rift developed between Yuan Tan and Yuan Shang. Yuan Tan stationed his army at Liyang, while Yuan Shang provided little military support to Yuan Tan but sent Feng Ji to accompany him. When Yuan Tan requested more troops, Shen Pei and others deliberated and decided not to provide them. Yuan Tan became enraged and killed Feng Ji. The Records of Heroes states: Feng Ji, courtesy name Yuantu. Initially, when Yuan Shao fled from Dong Zhuo, he traveled with Xu You and Feng Ji to Jizhou. Yuan valued Feng Ji's intelligence and strategic ability highly, trusting him greatly and working closely with him on important matters. Later, when Shen Pei gained influence, he did not get along well with Feng Ji. Someone once slandered Shen Pei to Yuan Shao, and when Yuan asked Feng Ji about it, Feng Ji said: "Shen Pei is by nature upright and resolute; he embodies the integrity of ancient men. He should not be doubted." Yuan Shao asked, "Do you not dislike him?" Feng Ji replied: "What we previously quarreled over was personal matters; what I now present is for the sake of state affairs." Yuan Shao appreciated this and ultimately did not remove Shen Pei. As a result, Shen Pei became closer to Feng Ji in friendship. Cao Cao crossed the river to attack Yuan Tan, and Yuan Tan urgently appealed for help from Yuan Shang. Yuan Shang wanted to send troops to aid Yuan Tan but feared that Yuan Tan might then seize his forces, so he left Shen Pei in charge of Ye and personally led an army to assist Yuan Tan. They confronted Cao Cao's forces at Liyang. From the second month to the ninth month, a major battle was fought beneath the city walls; Yuan Tan and Yuan Shang were defeated and retreated into the city for defense. Cao Cao was about to surround them when they fled under cover of night. He pursued them as far as Ye, harvested their grain, captured Yin'an, and then led his army back to Xu. Cao Cao launched a southern campaign against Jingzhou, advancing as far as Xiping. Yuan Tan and Yuan Shang then turned their forces against each other; Yuan Tan was defeated and fled to Pingyuan. Yuan Shang pressed his attack, so Yuan Tan sent Xin Bi to Cao Cao as an envoy requesting rescue. Cao Cao therefore returned to aid Yuan Tan and arrived at Liyang by the tenth month. The Wei Shi Chunqiu records a letter from Liu Biao to Yuan Tan, which said: "Heaven has inflicted great calamity; misfortune flows heavily. Your esteemed father passed away, and all under heaven mourn deeply." "Your virtuous descendants now inherit the legacy, and both near and far look to you with hope. All wish to contribute their strength in service of an alliance leader—this was your late father's desire even on his deathbed." "How could one foresee that green flies would swarm the banners, and Wuji would wander between both camps, causing trusted ministers to be divided into two factions, their loyalties severed as if they were separate bodies!" "In the past, among the Three Kings and Five Hegemons, down to the Warring States era, cases of fathers and sons turning against each other were not unheard of; yet these were either to achieve kingship, establish hegemony, honor one's ancestors, or secure the succession. There has never been a case where abandoning kin for another side and severing the very roots of one's lineage could lead to great achievements, lasting success, or enduring legacy passed down through generations." If Duke Xiang of Qi avenged a grudge from nine generations past, or Shi Gai completed the unfinished mission of Xun Yan, these were precisely the reasons why the Spring and Autumn Annals praised their righteousness, and gentlemen commended their faithfulness. The resentment of Bo You against Qi was not as intense as Tai Gong's anger toward Cao; the legacy carried on by Xuanzi was not comparable to the virtuous succession of a benevolent ruler. Moreover, gentlemen who avoid hardship do not flee to the land of their enemies. How could one forget the grievances of a late ruler and abandon the bonds with close kin? This would serve as a warning for future generations and bring disgrace upon an alliance! The arrogance of Jizhou's younger brother in defying his elder has already occurred; a benevolent ruler should humble himself and endure disgrace, regarding the restoration of the state as his foremost duty; although he may be hated by his wife, it is not comparable to Duke Zhuang of Zheng and Lady Jiang; the suspicion between brothers does not rival that between Zhonghua (Shun) and Xiang. Yet Duke Zhuang found joy in the Great Tunnel, while Xiang received a fief at Bi. I hope you will set aside past grievances and think deeply of our former bond, restoring the relationship between mother and son, brothers as it was in the beginning." He also sent a letter to Yuan Shang, saying: "I have learned that the conflict arose from Xin and Guo, leading to disaster among your own kin. You followed in the footsteps of Ebo and Shishen (mythical figures who fought each other), forgetting the meaning of brotherhood as expressed in the 'Changdi' poem. You turned on one another with weapons, leaving corpses and bloodshed; hearing this makes me choke with grief, for it feels as if you are already lost." In the past, Huangdi waged the Battle of Zhuolu and King Wu of Zhou led campaigns against Shang and Yan; these were all undertaken to eliminate corruption and establish royal authority—not struggles over strength or weakness, nor acts driven by anger or resentment. Therefore, even the destruction of one's own kin is not considered a fault, and killing an elder brother does not violate righteousness. Now, the two of you have just inherited a great legacy and continue the path laid by your predecessors. Moving forward, you face concerns over the peril of the state; retreating, you bear the regret left behind by former rulers. You should therefore devote yourselves solely to righteousness and ensure the well-being of the nation. Why is that? Metal, wood, water, and fire achieve harmony through a balance of rigidity and flexibility; only then can they be in harmony and serve the people. Now, Qingzhou is naturally sharp-tempered and confused about right and wrong. A benevolent ruler has a broad and generous mind, with ample capacity. You should embrace the small within the great and tolerate the inferior with grace. First eliminate Cao Cao to fulfill your late father's unfinished grudge; after the matter is settled, then discuss who was right or wrong—would this not be wise! If you focus on long-term plans and restrain yourself to restore propriety, then we should raise our banners and march forward together to support the royal house. But if you remain lost without returning to the right path or persist in your defiance without change, even the Hu and Yi (barbarian) peoples will have words of mockery and blame—how much more so among us allies? Could we still unite our strength to serve you then? This is like Han Lu and Dongguo trapping themselves in front, leaving the reward for the farmer. With anger rising like a crane gazing upward, Jizhou hopes to hear harmonious and united voices. If peace is achieved, then will the Yuan clan rise or fall with Han? If not, then our alliance will never have hope again." Tan and Shang both refused to follow. The Han Jin Chunqiu records that Shen Pei presented a letter to Tan, saying: "According to the principles of the Spring and Autumn Annals, when a national ruler dies for his state, loyal ministers die for their sovereign's command. If anyone plots to endanger the ancestral temple and disrupts the nation, undermining royal authority and established laws, then close or distant relatives are treated equally. Therefore, Duke of Zhou wept and concealed the case of Guan and Cai, while Ji You sighed deeply and carried out the poison against Zhen Shu. Why is that? Because righteousness outweighs personal sentiment, and the matter was unavoidable. In the past, Duke Ling of Wei deposed Kuaike and enthroned Zhe. Kuaike acted immorally, entered Qi to seize power, and was attacked by Wei's army. The Commentary on the Spring and Autumn Annals said: "According to the righteousness of Shi Manggu, it was justifiable to resist him." Therefore, Kuaike ultimately bore the guilt of rebellion, while Manggu forever enjoyed the reputation of a loyal minister. Even father and son are treated thus—how much more so brothers! In the past, our late father deposed General Kuaike to continue the line of his worthy elder brother and appointed you as the legitimate heir. He announced this above to the ancestors' spirits and below in official records. Our late father regarded you as his nephew, while you called him your uncle. This is known far and wide throughout the land—whose ears have not heard it? Moreover, on the day our late father passed away, you, General Yuan Tan, wore mourning clothes and dwelled in a hut of grief, while you, General Yuan Shang, observed your rites in a chamber for purification. The distinction between seniority and rank became even clearer at that time. At that time, the wicked minister Pang Ji recklessly added unnecessary actions like drawing a snake's foot, using twisted words and flattery to stir discord among close relatives. You, General Yuan Shang, rose in righteous anger and executed him without delay; I, General Yuan Tan, also carried out your orders and imposed cruel punishments upon him. Since then, the abscesses have burst and healed; there is not a trace of suspicion between flesh and blood. Even those who doubted themselves were able to preserve their lives and enjoy blessings. Therefore, we have dispatched all the strong Hu troops, selected renowned generals, prepared weapons and equipment, chosen warriors, exhausted the treasury's wealth, and drained the land's provisions. What has not been provided to supply you, General? Superiors and subordinates alike have led together to protect the banner; in battle, we formed orderly ranks like wild geese; in contributions, we served as the main source of tribute. Even if granaries were emptied and treasuries exhausted, and even if people's possessions were stripped away, all above and below rejoiced and supported this effort, none daring to complain of hardship. Why is that? Because the heartfelt loyalty of each person, and every household's most devoted plans, are like lips to teeth or a frame to its wheel—mutually supportive without need for reward. It was believed that you, General, shared the same heart and purpose, united as one body. You were expected to combine strength and equal power to defend against enemies and bring peace to our home. Who could have foreseen that wicked and slanderous men, fabricating baseless accusations and inciting treachery for personal gain, would lead you to suddenly change your course? You forgot the benevolence of filial piety and brotherly affection, heeded the schemes of wolves and jackals, falsely accused our late father of unjust depositions, violated the mourning rites of a close relative, defied the principles of discipline and order, disregarded the moral distinctions between right and wrong, and dared to replace the ruler of Jizhou in defiance, seeking to claim the position meant for our late father's rightful heir. Thus, you unleashed troops to pillage and plunder, slaughtered officials and civilians, leaving corpses piled in the fields and naked bodies scattered across the land. Some had their hair shorn or skin flayed, others suffered severed limbs; grieving souls cried out from the netherworld, while wounded survivors wailed among thorns and brambles. Furthermore, you plotted to seize the city of Ye, promising Qin and Hu treasures, women, and property in advance as rewards. Some heard you order officials and soldiers: "Even though I have an aged mother, I will only ensure her body remains intact." Those who heard these words were all shocked and breathless, their hearts filled with sorrow as they wept. This caused the Lady of the House to grieve and rage in her chamber, while my state's officials, ministers, and friends lay awake in grief, unable to know where to place their hands or feet; We wished to restrain our troops and remain silent, allowing you to decide the course of action, but we feared violating the principles of loyalty and duty recorded in the Spring and Autumn Annals. This would bring unforeseen calamity upon Lady Tai and destroy the noble legacy left by our late father. Moreover, the entire army was filled with righteous anger and personal resentment; I, General Yuan Tan had no choice but to act, leading us into the battle at Guantao. At that time, outwardly we were defending against hardship, but inwardly it was a plea for clemency. Yet no forgiveness was granted, and the Tu Ge wavered in their loyalty, betraying us on the battlefield. I, General Yuan Tan, found myself neither advancing nor retreating with success, attacked from both front and rear, was forced to retreat in flight, and dared not even say goodbye. I had hoped you, General, would show a little kindness to close kin and grant the mercy of leniency in pursuit. Instead, you followed our tracks relentlessly, leaving us no escape from death. A cornered beast must fight, and so we dared to defy your strict command. Yet your army crumbled like broken clay—this was not due to human strength but the will of Heaven itself. After this, we still hoped you, General, might change your ways and return to the path of self-restraint and propriety, restoring as before the affection between close brothers; yet you indulged your emotions and unleashed wrath, hastening the destruction of our family. You stood on tiptoe like a crane, linking with external enemies, scattering sharp weapons as if sparks in fire, spreading poison and harm far and wide. Smoke from beacons rose everywhere; blood stained the land for thousands of li. Cities were left in ruin, people suffering under hardship, all looking up in sorrowful lamentation. Though we wished not to intervene, how could we remain idle? Therefore, I led my troops eastward to defend the rightful borders. Though our camps were near the suburbs and did not encroach upon your territory, could one see the banners without sighing deeply? Shen Pei and others served as ministers of our late father, carrying out his command regarding depositions and enthronements. Yet figures like Tu disrupted the state and brought chaos to the family—this is a matter for established punishments under ritual law. Therefore, we must mobilize the resources of our impoverished state to remove this illness from you, General. If Heaven were to illuminate your heart and prompt you to act swiftly in punishment, then I, General Yuan Tan would crawl before you in sorrowful wails at your feet, while Shen Pei and others would lay bare their bodies to await the executioner's axe. If you remain unrepentant, then let the state perish for it—Tu's head will not hang from a pole, and our army will never retreat. I hope you, General, will carefully consider the matter and grant me this ring as a sign of finality." The Dianlue states: "Tan received the letter in sorrow, climbed onto the city wall and wept." Having been pressured by Guo Tu, and with repeated clashes of arms, they thus continued to fight without resolution. Shang heard that Taizu was marching northward and withdrew from Pingyuan back to Ye. His generals Lü Kuang and Lü Xiang defected from Shang to Taizu, and Tan secretly carved a general's seal to give to Kuang and Xiang. Taizu knew that Tan was deceiving him, so he arranged a marriage alliance to pacify him, then withdrew his troops. Shang sent Shen Pei and Su You to defend Ye, while again attacking Tan at Pingyuan. Taizu advanced his troops to attack Ye, reaching the Huan River fifty li from Ye. Su You had planned to serve as an inside ally but the plot was exposed. He fought Shen Pei within the city and was defeated, fleeing to Taizu instead. Taizu then advanced to attack them, digging underground tunnels; Shen Pei also dug trenches inside the city to counter them. Shen Pei's general Feng Li opened a sally port, allowing over three hundred of Taizu's soldiers to enter. Shen Pei discovered this and from the city walls dropped large stones onto the breach gate. The gate closed just in time, killing all who had entered. Taizu then encircled Ye and dug trenches stretching forty li around the city. At first, he ordered them to be shallow, appearing as if they could easily be crossed. Shen Pei looked at them and laughed, not sending out troops to contest the advantage. That night, Taizu dug them deeper and wider, making the trenches two zhang wide and deep, then diverted the Zhang River to flood them. From the fifth month until the eighth month, more than half of those in the city starved or died from hunger. Shang, hearing of the crisis at Ye, led more than ten thousand troops back to relieve it. Marching from the western mountains and arriving eastward at Yangpingting, seventeen li from Ye, he reached the Fu River, lit a signal fire to communicate with those in the city, who also responded by lighting fires. Shen Pei sent troops out from the northern part of the city, intending to meet Shang and break the siege together. Taizu launched a preemptive attack against them, defeating their forces who returned in retreat; Shang also suffered a defeat and fled to Quzhang to set up camp. Taizu then encircled him there. Before the forces could join, Shang grew fearful and sent Yin Kui and Chen Lin to request surrender, but Taizu refused to accept it. Shang fled back toward Lan Kou, but Taizu intensified the siege. Shang's generals Ma Yan and others defected on the battlefield; his forces collapsed in panic, and Shang fled to Zhongshan. Taizu seized all their supplies, capturing Shang's seals of office, ceremonial axes, and personal belongings to display at his residence. The people within the city lost morale and fell into despair. Shen Pei's nephew, Shen Rong, who was defending the eastern gate, opened it at night to let Taizu's troops in. Shen Pei fought them within the city but was captured alive. Shen Pei spoke with bold and resolute spirit, never uttering a word of submission; those who saw him could not help but sigh in admiration. He was then executed. The Xianxian Xingzhuang records: "Shen Pei, courtesy name Zhengnan, was a native of Wei Jun. From his youth he was loyal and valiant, displaying an unyielding sense of integrity that could not be violated." Yuan Shao governed Jizhou and entrusted him with the most important responsibilities, appointing him as Zhizhong Biejia (a high-ranking administrative official) and overseeing the entire military headquarters. Initially, when Tan left, he called for Xinchang and Guo Tu's families to be released from their homes, but the family of Xin Ping was uniquely arrested. When Shen Pei's nephew opened the city gate to let in Taizu's troops, Shen Pei was on a watchtower at the southeast corner of the city. Seeing Taizu's forces enter, he was furious that Xin Pi and Guo Tu had ruined Jizhou, so he sent someone quickly to Ye Prison to order the execution of Zhongzhi's family. At that time, Xin Pi was in the army and upon hearing of the gate being opened, rushed to the prison to save his brother's family, but they had already been executed. On that day, Shen Pei was captured alive and led toward the command tent. Xin Pi and others intercepted him and struck his head with a horsewhip, cursing at him: "You wretch! You are truly dead today!" Shen Pei turned and said, "You curs! It was precisely because of you that Jizhou fell. I wish I could kill you!" "And do you think you can decide my life or death today?" After a while, Taizu summoned him and asked Shen Pei: "Do you know who opened your city gate for me?" Shen Pei replied, "I do not know." Taizu said: "It was your own nephew Shen Rong!" Shen Pei said, "My foolish son is not worth using to the point of this!" Taizu then added: "Earlier when I was on my hunting expedition, why were there so many crossbows?" Shen Pei replied: "I regretted that they were too few!" Taizu said, "You remained loyal to the Yuan family father and son; you had no choice but to do so." He had a desire to spare his life. Since Shen Pei showed no sign of submission and Xin Pi and others continued to weep and wail, he was executed. Earlier, Zhang Ziqian of Jizhou had surrendered first and had always been on poor terms with Shen Pei. He sneered at Shen Pei and said: "Zhengnan, how do you compare to me now?" Shen Pei shouted angrily: "You are a surrendered slave, while Shen Pei is a loyal minister. Though I die, how can I be compared to you who lives in shame?" As he was about to be executed, he shouted at the soldiers holding weapons and ordered them to face northward, saying: "My lord is in the north." The Records of the Shanyang Gong by Yue Zi and The Spring and Autumn Annals of Emperor Xian by Yuan Wei both state that Taizu's troops entered the city, and Shen Pei fought in the gate. After being defeated, he fled into a well and was captured there. I, Songzhi, believe that Shen Pei was a loyal and valiant man of his generation, a faithful minister to the Yuan family. How could he, on the day when all hope had been lost, have fled into a well to save himself? This is hard to believe and indeed easily understood as untrue. I do not know who Yue Zi, Yuan Wei and others actually were; they failed to discern the truth or falsehood of such matters, yet lightly manipulated their pens, fabricating strange accounts at will in order to promote their own writings. Such cases are precisely sufficient to deceive the public and mislead future generations. They are indeed criminals in the annals of history, people whom true scholarship would not accept. Gao Gan surrendered with Bingzhou and was again appointed as governor of Bingzhou.
